Modern Family takes product placement to a new level with plot paid for by National Association of Realtors

Modern Family have taken product placement to a whole new level after they featured a plot paid for by the National Association of Realtors.

The show's amiable but goofy dad, Phil Dunphy, plays up the fact that he's a Realtor in Wednesday's episode as part of new campaign by the Association.

Fearing traditional advertising is lost on a generation who frequently stream shows ad-free, the group had paid an undisclosed fee to the Emmy award-winning show on ABC to make the distinction between Realtors and real estate agents.

National Association of Realtors are also featuring Ty Burrell, playing his Modern Family character Phil Dumphy, in a series of traditional ads

The episode, aired on Wednesday, sees Dunphy team up with Gloria Pritchett to win a battle against her nemesis by confronting her with insider knowledge gleaned from his profession: that there's been illegal construction on the woman's property, and her grandson is attending an out-of-district school.

At the show's end, Dunphy straightens an 'R'' insignia pinned to his suit jacket.

'We have done only a handful of integrations because they tend to be intrusive and frankly make the show look like it's selling out,' Christopher Lloyd, co-executive producer of Modern Family, told Adweek. 

'This was actually one of the easier ones we've done, since it lent itself to a joke. Phil is bemoaning his career choice as a Realtor, and it's his Realtor skills that ultimately save the day.'

Lloyd said he would not have gone ahead 'if it seemed like we were just wedging in a pro-Realtor's Association testimonial. We only agreed to do it when we thought of a way to actually use the distinction between what a (NAR-certified) Realtor is and what a real-estate agent is in a funny way.'

The Realtors are touting their services over licensed agents who are not members of their association, and trying to counter a trend where more consumers are doing their own real estate research to avoid paying fees, said Elizabeth Mendenhall, incoming president of the association.

With Dunphy, portrayed by actor Ty Burrell, the association sensed an opportunity: a main character on one of television's most popular comedies who sells real estate (pictured in season one of Modern Family)

In last night's episode, Phil is bemoaning his career choice but is able to save the day through his skills as a Realtor (pictured from season one)

With Dunphy, portrayed by actor Ty Burrell, the group sensed an opportunity: a main character on one of television's most popular comedies who sells real estate.

'He's now one of America's best-loved Realtors,' Mendenhall said.

Neither Mendenhall nor ABC would say how much the association paid ABC for the placement. 

The campaign also includes more traditional ads featuring Burrell's character that premiered during two other ABC sitcoms Wednesday.

Burrell said there's always some nervousness when advertising intrudes on the creative process. But he said he trusted the 'Modern Family' executive producers, who signed off on the use and wrote the scenes. 'Their sense of caretaking for the show is very high,' he said.

The Realtors were given the chance to review what was written before the show aired, ABC said.

Mendenhall said the Realtors were not nervous about having their message carried by a character who, while likable, is often portrayed as bumbling and clueless. The Realtors want to convey that their members are just like anyone else in a community.

'We are real people helping real individuals and providing them with a part of the American dream,' she said. 'The fact that he is approachable is part of what makes him special.'

Burrell said it is clever advertising.

'I've always admired a company that allows themselves to be seen in an imperfect light or allows themselves to be poked fun at,' he said. 'That's the strongest play.'

In the traditional ads, screened during breaks in ABC shows and on cable networks like HGTV that feature real estate-oriented programs, Dunphy disseminates his 'Phil-osophies.' 

In one ad, Dunphy sits beside a fire and talks, incessantly, about the important role of silence in a negotiation.

Another funny advert sees him explaining that 'navigating the real-estate world requires cat-like reflexes.' Of course, in classic Dunphy style, the demonstration of his reflexes, does not quite go to plan.

The 'Phil's-osophies' approach, 'allows us to get a little more specific about the ways a Realtor provides an advantage,' said Sean McBride, executive creative director at Arnold.. 'And we'll be extending the campaign into social in the coming months, which is another great place to find our millennial audience.' 

'Those of us who've actually bought a home know the process is rife with unexpected pitfalls. So, this integration provides us a way to reinforce the value of a Realtor to a captive audience, in a context that's relevant to them.'

It opened on Dunphy family matriarch Claire's laptop, sat in Chicago's O'Hare airport waiting for a flight back to Los Angeles.

She was trying to reach her daughter Haley over video message when other members of the family start popping in to say 'hi' on her computer screen.

The episode, which was filmed on iPhones, iPads, and Macbook pros, aimed to show the real way most relatives communicate today thanks to their high-tech devices, according to show producer Steve Levitan. 

Christopher Lloyd, co-executive producer said he would not have gone ahead 'if it seemed like we were just wedging in a pro-Realtor's Association testimonial'

But, while Modern Family asked Apple's permission to feature their products, the ABC series were never paid by the multinational firm.

The collaboration with National Association of Realtors is the first paid-for integration of products within the show. The fee has been revealed.

Advertisers have been turning to product placement in response to more and more viewers watching shows and films online through services such as Netflix.

Currently product placement account for just two per cent of global advertising, but with viewers watching more ad-free content, Branded Entertainment Network, or BEN, says 'embedded' advertising has become crucial for brands.

'Entertainment is advertising's next frontier,' Gary Shenk, BEN chief executive, told the Financial Times.

'Viewers are tuning out of ad-supported formats,' he said. 'Most advertising never gets watched, so being embedded in the content is absolutely critical. We want to turn premium entertainment into advertising and do it in a scalable way.'

While TV may be the next 'frontier', product placement within movies are becoming increasingly common — and more lucrative.

Last year, Bond movie, Spectre, featured more products than any other 007 offering with at least 17 brands plying their wares on screen.

Ranging from Tom Ford suits and Heineken beer to Bollinger champagne and Sony mobile phones, a wide assortment of companies are set to cash in on the secret agent's enduring appeal.

One of the more blatant scenes sees Bond handed an Omega watch by Q only for the camera to zoom in on the item, clearly making sure viewers could see the brand name.

Bond star Daniel Craig later defended the number of product placements, claiming the films would otherwise be too expensive to make.

Michael Bay's Transformers: Age of Extinction, The Lego Movie and Captain America: The Winter Soldier have also been noted for their number of product placements.

But the award for sheer number of brands appearing in a movie goes to Superman film Man Of Steel which had astonishing 94 commercial deals when it opened in 2013, more than for any other film in history. 

Experts suggest they made Warner Bros more than £100 million.

Sharp-eyed cinema-goers will notice that Superman, played by the British actor Henry Cavill, likes to use a Nokia phone. 

The native of the planet Krypton also drinks Budweiser and shops at Sears. And those cars tossed about in the action scenes? Spookily, they always seem to be Chryslers.
