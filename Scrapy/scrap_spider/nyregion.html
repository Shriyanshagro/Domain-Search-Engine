Pataki Signs Sex-Crime Bill Modeled on 'Megan's Law'

By JAMES DAO
Published: July 26, 1995

ALBANY, July 25— Gov. George E. Pataki signed legislation today that will require convicted sex offenders to notify the state about where they are living long after they are released from prison.

The legislation, modeled roughly after New Jersey's "Megan's Law," will also make detailed information about convicted sex offenders available to the public through a "900" telephone number and will authorize police departments to disseminate information on violent sexual felons to their communities.

Several parents of murdered children joined Mr. Pataki, a Republican, in the signing ceremony, including Maureen Kanka, whose daughter, Megan, inspired the New Jersey law.

"This takes back the old standard of the criminal always having the rights," Mrs. Kanka said. "It's a first step to giving rights to our children."

Civil libertarians immediately denounced the law, saying it would violate the constitutional rights of sex offenders by imposing new sanctions on people who have already paid for their crimes. They also warned that the legislation would encourage acts of vigilantism against ex-convicts and could lead to innocent people being mistakenly branded sex offenders.

"Under our laws, a person is subject to notice about what behavior is prohibited and what the penalties are," Donna Lieberman of the New York Civil Liberties Union said. "The government can't change the rules after the individual has already been convicted."

The bill's authors, Assemblyman Daniel L. Feldman, Democrat of Brooklyn, and Senator Dean G. Skelos, Republican of Nassau County, said they tailored the legislation to meet constitutional concerns raised in legal attacks on New Jersey's law. The New Jersey Supreme Court upheld that state's law today, but it is still facing a challenge in Federal court.

Mr. Feldman said the New York law does not require local authorities to disseminate information about convicted sex offenders, as the New Jersey law does. He said lower courts have ruled that the New Jersey law, in requiring such dissemination, was unconstitutionally punitive. New York's version, he said, will simply fulfill a "regulatory function" by making information available to the public.

"The issue here has always been whether or not we trust the people of this state to know what the state already knows," Mr. Feldman said. "By and large the experience has been that we can."

Under the new law, people convicted of sex offenses -- including rape, sexual assault and child molestation -- will be required to register their addresses with the state upon being released from prison, and annually thereafter. Violent sex offenders will be required to continue registering for the rest of their lives; nonviolent offenders will have to register for 10 years.

The law, which takes effect in January, will apply to as many as 10,000 people now serving prison terms and another 5,000 on parole or probation. The state estimates that 1,200 people are convicted of sex offenses annually.

The state will also establish a computer data base on all convicted sex offenders. It will include their addresses, physical descriptions, criminal histories and modes of operation.

Upon sentencing or release from prison, judges will be required to designate sex offenders to one of three categories: high, moderate or low risk, depending on the violence of their crimes and their estimated propensity to repeat them.

The data bank will be available to the public by calling a 900 number. To gain access, a caller will have to have specific information about a person, including a name and a Social Security number, address or some other identifying detail.

The amount of information to be released will depend on the risk category of the convict. All information, including addresses, will be released for high-risk felons, less for moderate-risk ones. Information on low-risk offenders will be available only to the police.

Police departments will also be allowed to disseminate information on high-risk offenders through, say, leaflets or news releases.

Despite the screening procedure, Ms. Lieberman called the 900 number "a recipe for disaster" because it could cause people with names and addresses similar to convicts to be branded as sex offenders. "This is tailor-made for giving out wrong information," she said.
