The rear that makes £36m a year...with a little bit of help from a sex tape, a pushy mother and an insatiable desire for fame

  • This book looks at the remarkable rise of the Kardashian empire 
  • Expose goes behind the scenes to uncover the secrets to their fame
  • Ian Halperin argues that sex tape starring Kim was leaked by the family 

By Christopher Stevens for Daily Mail

Published: 22:35 GMT, 5 May 2016 | Updated: 23:15 GMT, 5 May 2016

Book of the week

KARDASHIAN DYNASTY

by Ian Halperin

(Simon & Schuster £16.99)

On a marble table in the foyer of the California house owned by Kris Jenner, matriarch of the Kardashian clan, a pile of papers is stacked. These are non-disclosure agreements [NDAs], legally binding documents, the showbiz equivalent of the Official Secrets Act, and they have to be signed by everyone who walks into the house.

Above the table hangs a piece of twee doggerel in a frame: ‘What we say here, what we see here, let it stay here, when we leave here.’ To breach an NDA would be to invite a catastrophic law suit.

Bottom line: Kim Kardashian, who is married to Kanye West, is highly skilled at promoting herself

As one Hollywood producer put it to author Ian Halperin, this would be ‘career suicide - and very expensive’.

This explains why Halperin, a sensation-hungry writer who has enjoyed bestsellers with his melodramatic theories about the deaths of Michael Jackson and Nirvana’s Kurt Cobain, has been unable to persuade anyone involved in the reality TV show Keeping Up With The Kardashians to talk with him on the record: not one cameraman, not a single make-up artist, not even a delivery driver. They all obeyed the code of silence.

Halperin’s response was to write a different sort of expose, one that reveals the rot within the Kardashian empire. To hear him tell it, most convincingly, the world of Kris and her daughters - Kourtney, Kim, Khloe, Kendall and Kylie - is a veneer of glamour over a fetid mound of dirt.

The show screens in Britain and the U.S. on the E! cable network, with new episodes airing on Sunday nights since 2007 and repeats showing almost constantly. At its peak, it has attracted more than ten million viewers in the States.

You might never have seen it, you might swear you never will. But you have heard the name Kardashian, because their thirst for fame pervades the planet.

Kim, who is married to rap superstar Kanye West, is highly skilled at promoting herself: her appearance on India’s version of Big Brother in 2014 introduced her to 150 million new viewers, for instance.

But all the sisters are brilliantly adept at using social media, and especially the power of the ‘selfie’, to gain attention.

KARDASHIAN DYNASTY by Ian Halperin

This is a family whose fame is built on the success of a sex tape made in 2003. It starred Kim, who was then in her early 20s . . . and may well have been peddled to internet distributors by her mother.

The family deny leaking it deliberately, but Halperin makes a strong case that no one else could be behind its release.

That tape turned the clan from small-time hustlers into multi-millionaire celebrities. As a ploy to attract media attention, it was more successful than anyone could have foreseen: until then, Kim had been a hanger-on, trailing round after her friend Paris Hilton. Was it Hilton’s own sex tape that inspired Kim’s?

If this short-cut to wealth and notoriety appeals to you, buy this book. It includes an entire chapter on the process of marketing sex tapes, with Halperin posing as a middle-man hawking an imaginary video of a basketball star and a model.

He investigates the legal loopholes, tests the credulity of the marketeers, and does everything but provide templates of contracts for wannabe celebs to fill in and sign. Unless you have some kind of actual talent - the ability to sing or dance or act (which Kim and her siblings certainly don’t) - the alternative to a sex tape appears to be a lifetime of obscurity. Kris had been striving for years to achieve real fame, until her daughter became an internet porn sensation.

Her only brush with stardom had come when her ex-husband, Robert, joined the legal team of his friend O.J. Simpson during the former athlete’s trial for the murder of his ex-wife Nicole and her friend, Ron Goldman.

Robert Kardashian made his fortune on the fringes of the music industry, and was starstruck all his life. During the Seventies he had an affair with Priscilla Presley, after she left Elvis. In the Eighties, after he married Kris, Robert would throw extravagant parties to surround himself with A-listers such as George Michael and Magic Johnson. Kris loved the lifestyle, but she was also highly sexed.

According to one rumour, she had an affair with O.J. while still married to Robert. It is even said, though the family scoff at the suggestion, that O.J. is the father of Kris’s third daughter, Khloe . . . the one who looks nothing like her sisters.

Halperin boasts that he has watched more than 200 hours of the show and its spin-offs. To prove it, the second half of the book describes in turgid detail the main plot points

As played by former Friends actor David Schwimmer in the recent BBC 2 serial The People v O.J. Simpson, Robert Kardashian, with his dramatic grey kiss-curl, was a mild and nervous man.

In reality, he was prone to outbursts of anger. One of Kris’s ex-lovers tells Halperin that the lawyer once attacked his car with a golf-club, and says Kris was afraid he might shoot them.

After the Kardashian marriage fell apart, Kris turned to an Olympic gold medallist, the former decathlete Bruce Jenner - much better known by the Nineties for his shameless product endorsements and seedy ad deals than for his sporting achievements, but he never managed to make any money.

When Kris met him, he was living in a one-room bungalow and was behind on the rent - but she recognised a kindred spirit, one willing to exploit whatever was left of his reputation. She imagined Bruce Jenner fitness equipment, vitamin supplements and sports clothes, and set about making a TV infomercial called Superfit With Kris And Bruce Jenner.

Her second daughter, Kim, had inherited her acumen. As a teenager, desperate to outshop her friends but short of cash, she set up an eBay account and sold everything in her wardrobe that she didn’t expect to wear again.

Then she borrowed money from her father to purchase five pairs of Manolo Blahnik shoes, guessing that they would soon be collectable. She sold them at a 350 per cent profit.

Naturally, her friends were envious - so she offered to sell off their wardrobes, too, for a percentage of the profits.

Today, her earnings are astonishing. Last year, Kim grossed $53.5m (£36 m). The internet is obsessed with her. When she posed, bare-bottomed, for the little-known Paper magazine, one per cent of the next day’s web traffic in the U.S. consisted of people rushing to see that picture - spawning the slogan, ‘Break The Internet, Kim Kardashian!’

If she was born with her mother’s business sense, Kim also had her father’s weakness for famous names. Her first serious boyfriend and lover, when she was 14, was Tito Joe Jackson, a nephew of Michael. That year, she had her birthday party at Jackson’s Neverland ranch.

Her sex tape co-starred Ray Norwood, brother of the R&B singer Brandy. Halperin prints the rumour, strenuously denied by Kim, that she gave Brandy’s American Express card number to her sisters, who contrived to run up unauthorised bills of $120,000 (£82,500).

That might be untrue, but it certainly marries the twin Kardashian traits of celebrity worship and rampant materialism.

When Kris saw how lucrative her daughter’s porn career could be, she urged her to do a nude Playboy shoot - and then, for good measure, posed herself, wrapped naked in the American flag.

The resultant publicity had its effect, and the first series of the family’s scripted reality show, Keeping Up With The Kardashians, followed.

It purports to document their day-to-day home life. The truth is much more cynical and manufactured, of course - the women act out rows and crises. It’s no more, really, than showing off for the camera, but it earns them worldwide audiences of tens of millions.

Halperin boasts that he has watched more than 200 hours of the show and its spin-offs. To prove it, the second half of the book describes in turgid detail the main plot points. Since he has already established it’s basically fake, this seems superfluous.

He touches on Bruce’s ‘gender transition’ to become Caitlyn Jenner, without doing much more than running through the headlines. He also hints that Kris has developed a drink problem since Bruce left, though he presents no evidence.

In the end, the book is very like one of the Kardashian shows: switch off halfway through and you won’t miss much.
