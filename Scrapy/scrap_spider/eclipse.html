What Does the Magnitude of an Eclipse Mean?

The magnitude of an eclipse is the fraction of the diameter of the disc of the eclipsed body that is covered by the eclipsing body.

  • The magnitude of a solar eclipse is the fraction of the Sun's disc covered by the Moon.
  • The magnitude of a lunar eclipse is the fraction of the Moon covered by the Earth’s shadow. Because the Earth casts two different shadows – the umbra and the penumbra – there are two magnitudes associated with lunar eclipses: the penumbral magnitude and the umbral magnitude.

The value of the magnitude of an eclipse – solar or lunar – is expressed in terms of a fraction (0.75) or a percentage (75%).

Most scientific and astronomical outlets report the magnitude of an eclipse at the greatest or maximum point of the eclipse.

Magnitude of a Solar Eclipse

The magnitude of an annular or partial solar eclipse is between 0 and 1, and it is always less than 1. The magnitude of a total solar eclipse is always equal to or greater than 1.

The magnitude of a solar eclipse varies from one location to another and from the beginning of the eclipse to the end of the eclipse at a location.

Total Solar Eclipse: Higher Magnitude = Longer Totality

Total solar eclipses with a magnitude of more than 1 tend to have longer totality, and they are usually visible from a larger region of the Earth. On the other hand, phenomena such as Bailey's beads, the Sun's chromosphere and shadow bands tend to be visible only for a short time during these types of eclipses.

The opposite occurs when a total solar eclipse's magnitude is 1 or just slightly above 1. Such eclipses tend to have a shorter totality and are usually visible from a smaller portion of Earth. However, observers have an extended view of phenomena that occur during totality.

Imitating Total Eclipses

Annular eclipses that have a magnitude close to 1, tend to look like total solar eclipses – the skies get darker, the temperature falls considerably, and phenomena that occur during a total eclipse can sometimes be observed.

Lunar Eclipses: Two Magnitudes

There are two magnitudes associated with a lunar eclipse – an umbral magnitude and a penumbral magnitude. The umbral magnitude is the fraction of the Moon's diameter that is covered by the Earth's umbra at the instance of the greatest eclipse.

A partial lunar eclipse has an umbral magnitude that is greater than 0 and less than 1 while a total lunar eclipse always has an umbral magnitude greater than 1. A penumbral lunar eclipse, on the other hand, has negative values for its umbral magnitude.

Penumbral Magnitude

The penumbral magnitude is the fraction of the Moon's diameter that is covered by the Earth's penumbra – the lighter part of the Earth's shadow. The penumbral magnitude of a total lunar eclipse is always greater than 2 while the penumbral magnitude of a partial lunar eclipse is always greater than 1 and less than 2.

When the penumbral magnitude of an eclipse is less than 1, only a small part of the Moon's diameter is covered by the Earth's shadow. This happens during a penumbral lunar eclipse. Such eclipses are very hard to view by observers on Earth. In fact, penumbral eclipses with a magnitude of less than 0.60 usually go undetected by the naked eye.

Not to be Confused with Obscuration

Oftentimes, the eclipse magnitude is confused with the eclipse obscuration, which is the fraction of the area of the eclipsed body obscured by the eclipsing body during an eclipse. In the case of a solar eclipse, it is the fraction of the Sun's area that is covered by the disc of the Moon. Like eclipse magnitude, eclipse obscuration is also expressed in terms of fractions (0.75) or percentages (75%).

As a rule when the solar eclipse is partial or annular, its obscuration is always less than its magnitude. For instance, the magnitude for the Annular Solar Eclipse on September 1, 2016, is 0.9736, while the obscuration is 0.9479.

The obscuration of a total solar eclipse, on the other hand, is always greater than its magnitude. Take, for example, the next total solar eclipse visible in mainland United States on August 21, 2017. The obscuration of the eclipse at its greatest point will be 1.0621, while its magnitude will be 1.0306. This eclipse is being called the Great American Eclipse of 2017.

Eclipse Magnitude Isn't Apparent Magnitude

The magnitude of a solar eclipse should also not be confused with the apparent magnitude of a celestial object. In astronomy, the apparent magnitude of a celestial body measures the brightness of the object as observed from Earth.

First devised by Greek astronomer Hipparchus around 150 BCE, the apparent magnitude scale was refined by astronomers with the advent of high power telescopes. Under this scale, which is logarithmic and backward, the apparent magnitude of a body is a measure of how bright it looks when it is at its greatest distance from the Earth. The larger the magnitude of a celestial object, the fainter it looks to observers on Earth.

Eight Elements

The magnitude of a solar eclipse is calculated using its Besselian elements. Introduced by Prussian mathematician and astronomer Friedrich Bessel in 1824 as a way to predict the characteristics of solar eclipses, Besselian elements are a group of variables that describe the movement of the Moon over the fundamental plane.

The fundamental plane is an imaginary plane that passes through the center of the Earth and is perpendicular to the axis of the shadow of the Moon. The axis of the lunar shadow is the imaginary line that connects the centers of the Sun, which is the eclipsed body, and the Moon, which is the eclipsing body.

There are 8 Besselian elements, each of which can be used to calculate the features of a solar eclipse, including the timing of the different stages of the eclipse, the duration of totality and the eclipse's magnitude.
