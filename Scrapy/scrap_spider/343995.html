Identacor

  • editor rating: fair
  • Comments
  • April 29, 2016
  • REVIEW
  • Comments
  • SPECS
  • COMPARE
MSRP
$1.00
  • Pros

    Desktop single sign-on (SSO) allows you to keep authentication traffic local when a request initiates inside a corporate network. Entry-level price point very reasonable, particularly with SaaS provisioning.

  • Cons

    Manual group management completely wrong for streamlining cloud app management. No support for LDAP directories. Multi-factor options very limited.

  • Bottom Line

    Identacor is missing too many features for us to consider it a prime contender in the identity management space. Dynamic groups are the most glaring omission, but a lack of options for multi-factor authentication (MFA) and even support for third-party LDAP directories are other notable areas in need of work.

By Tim Ferrill

Identacor (which begins at $1 per user per month) began life with a familiar anecdote: The founders encountered a glaring business need and realized they had the technical talents to fill it. Unfortunately, Identacor's current feature set and focus have a lot more to do with those specific needs, mainly revolving around single sign-on (SSO), rather than what's been identified as important in the Identity-as-a-Service (IDaaS) space in general. The result is a product with a great price and some innovative features, but one that's lacking several key components you can find in most of the competition, especially Editors' Choice winner Okta Identity Management .

Setup and Directory Integration
Identacor's initial setup process involves the creation of a trial account, some basic setup steps such as naming your instance, and configuring the subdomain (YourCompany.startsso.com). Populating your user base can be accomplished either through connecting to an existing directory (covered in the next section), importing a CSV file, or manually creating users one at a time, which we'd consider prohibitive for all but the smallest organizations.

We consider the ability to connect an existing corporate directory to an identity provider as mandatory for an IDaaS product, though different offerings handle this in different ways. All the solutions we've reviewed offer an Active Directory (AD) agent, and Identacor is no exception. Enabling the AD agent is as simple as installing the tool and entering your Identacor customer domain, username, and password.

Once you've connected Identacor to your AD domain you can configure options like the field used to create Identacor usernames; though this is limited to the email address, the user principal name (UPN), or the SAM account name. You also have the option to configure the action to be taken when a user's AD account is deleted.

However, a key feature that needs to be improved is the ability to target the AD users imported into Identacor. At the time of this review, this feature is limited to selecting Organizational Units, even though the OU selection list doesn't appear to get updated very frequently, which caused it to become out of sync with changes made in our test AD structure. Ideally, we'd like to see some flexibility here, including the ability to filter based on a user's group membership or attributes. We're told this feature is in the works, along with the ability to automatically provision accounts in Identacor rather than requiring an administrator action for imported users.

Most identity providers, particularly those in the upper echelon like Okta Identity Management and OneLogin, can also connect to standard Lightweight Directory Access Protocol (LDAP) directories or other common cloud identity repositories, like Google Apps or Office 365. Unfortunately, Identacor doesn't even yet offer LDAP support, which is practically ubiquitous for the rest of the field. You do have the option of configuring Security Assertion Markup Language (SAML) authentication allowing you to link your Identacor instance with another identity provider, but that's far less efficient than offering LDAP connectivity outright.

User Provisioning
We've already mentioned that Identacor offers several methods to create users, including an AD connector, CSV imports, and trusted identity providers through SAML. Clearly, creating an account in Identacor isn't supposed to be the endgame. The whole point of an IDaaS or on-premises identity management tool is to enable you to more efficiently manage your users and their access to other applications, network resources, and services.

Identity managers need to aggregate or group users based on features of the underlying directory, usually AD, such as user attributes or group memberships. Typically, an IDaaS will either synchronize AD groups and memberships, or it'll allow you to populate a separate group structure within the identity provider's instance in order to group users and provide access to SaaS apps and similar resources. Unfortunately Identacor doesn't currently offer either of these solutions, which is a deal breaker for all but the smallest use cases. Without the ability to dynamically assign users to their SaaS apps based on things that are already being managed in your organization, there's not much added in terms of efficiency.

Single Sign-On (SSO) and Reporting
Single sign-on (SSO) seems to be a key focus for Identacor, and it offers several ways for users to access the apps assigned to them. There's a web-based SSO portal, browser plugins, and mobile apps for Android and iOS. The SSO portal has a tabbed view that allows for some level of user customization, and can also tell apps to launch automatically when the user signs into the portal. The mobile apps allow users to access apps through the portal, but doesn't offer any additional features such as operating as a second factor for authentication, like Centrify Identity Service does.

Speaking of multi-factor authentication (MFA), Identacor supports prompting users with security questions and picture categories they need to identify correctly in order to complete the authentication process. These are somewhat outdated methods, yet the only traditional multi-factor provider supported by Identacor at this time is Google Authenticator. Again, this will almost certainly improve as Identacor matures.

One other piece Identacor offers to the SSO puzzle is a tool called Desktop SSO. Identacor allows you to install the Desktop SSO agent on a local server in order to handle SSO authentication in your corporate network without having to send authentication traffic across the internet. That's a nice add, though not enough to make up for all the omissions we've cataloged so far.

Even reporting, which is also a well-established feature set amongst the competition, is a weak spot. Identacor really only offers a couple of tools which fall under the reporting category. The Dashboard, which isn't available at the entry-level pricing tier, provides a few key performance indicators (KPIs) and shows a list of recent activity. Also available are activity reports, which allow you to filter the activity log down to specific date ranges and activity types, as well as offering the ability to export the report to a CSV file. The activity report covers the minimum for an IDaaS service, but isn't a comprehensive solution by any means and Identacor will need to step up its game here to appeal to even midsized business customers.

Low Price Doesn't Compensate
If you're a small business that doesn't really care about the gaps between AD and your SaaS apps within Identacor, then the basic pricing of $1 per user per month will be enticing, particularly considering this tier includes support for provisioning users into SaaS apps. But the basic tier only supports the configuration of 10 apps and 5 groups, so keep that in mind as your company starts growing.

For customers that need MFA and AD integration, you'll need to bump up to the Pro service level, which will set you back $5 per user each month. The Pro tier supports up to 100 apps and 20 groups. Identacor does offer an Enterprise tier that supports unlimited apps and groups as well as more hands-on support, but pricing has to be negotiated directly and individually, so your mileage will vary.

Overall, Identacor needs to mature to effectively compete with the established leaders in the identity management market. Our biggest issue has to do with the practical aspects of managing user app assignment without the ability to dynamically group them. The lack of MFA options is another reason to look for a more comprehensive solution. Both of these features are among th primary reasons organization go looking for identity management solutions, and Identacor lags behind most of the competition in both categories. Until the company addresses these areas, Identacor is really only suitable for the smallest organizations.
